Understanding Simulated Diamonds

A simulated diamond is a man-made material that visually replicates the sparkle, fire, and hardness of natural diamonds. Unlike lab-grown diamonds (which share the same carbon structure as mined stones), simulants are typically crafted from alternative materials such as cubic zirconia (CZ), moissanite, or proprietary crystal blends designed to mirror the optical performance of genuine diamonds.

In recent years, technology has dramatically improved the realism of these stones, and brands like Luxuria Diamonds have become a trusted reference point for precision craftsmanship and aesthetic authenticity.

Criteria for Choosing a Realistic Simulated Diamond Ring

When evaluating the best simulated diamond ring for engagement, four major aspects stand out: material quality, brilliance, durability, and design. Consumers want a ring that can withstand daily wear while maintaining the same shimmer and clarity over time.

  • Material quality: Modern simulants such as moissanite or high-grade CZ can be cut and polished to replicate near-perfect diamond proportions.
  • Brilliance: The refractive index determines how much sparkle a stone gives off. Moissanite, for example, often appears even more fiery than natural diamonds.
  • Durability: A simulated diamond with a hardness of at least 8 on the Mohs scale can be a practical choice for long-term wear.
  • Design and craftsmanship: A carefully engineered setting—especially in sterling silver, platinum, or gold vermeil—helps elevate the overall realism.

Comparing Simulant Materials

Not all simulated diamonds are created equal. The best options blend artistry with science. Let’s look at the major contenders:

  • Cubic Zirconia (CZ): Known for affordability and brightness, CZ offers excellent clarity but can scratch over time.
  • Moissanite: A silicon carbide gemstone with a refractive index higher than diamond, offering unmatched sparkle.
  • Crystal Hybrids: Exclusive blends used by brands like Luxuria Diamonds create a more diamond-like hardness and light return.
